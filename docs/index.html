Are California’s farmers planting different crops during the drought?

A story by Teresa Liu

Agriculture is one of the most impacted sectors during a drought. Roughly 40 percent of California’s water use is agricultural. Farmers have to make tough decisions about the future of their operations as water deliveries slowed down. For this project, I want to see whether farmers are ripping up certain crops in favor of ones that use less water.

I chose Tulare County for the analysis, because the county, located in California’s Central Valley, is one of the top producers of agricultural commodities in the country. More than 120 different crops are harvested in the region each year, according to Tulare County’s annual crop report.

California's 10 most water-intensive crops

California's various crops are commonly grouped into 20 categories. In 2010, the most recent year for which water usage data are available, alfalfa was the largest consumer.

These commodities are considered water-intensive, or crops that require significant amount of water to grow that could be saved for other uses. In the first chart below, I listed California’s top 10 most water intensive crops in a table. Many of them are also produced in Tulare County.

In the second chart, I used small multiples to show how much of each crop is produced in the county from 2010 to 2020. I analyzed 19 different crops. For the visualization, I chose to focus on just six crops: alfalfa, almonds, grapes, oranges, pomegranates and tangerines.

The period 2010 to 2020 is significant because we have had two droughts in the last decade. The first one stretched from 2012 to 2016, with 2012 to 2014 being the driest period in at least 1200 years. California has been under another drought since February 2020.

Alfalfa, which tops the water-intensive list, is cultivated as a vital forage crop eaten by grazing livestock. It’s an important resource for regions such as Tulare County, which is also a big producer of milk, cattles and calves.

Annual production of this crop fell almost 25 percent in 2013 and 32 percent in 2016. It dropped 14 percent in 2017, 11 percent in 2019 and 13 percent in 2020. Its cultivation jumped 51 percent in 2015, according to my analysis.

Alfalfa has a high rate of water use. It’s tolerant of drought but not drought resistant. Alfalfa can survive periodic droughts due to its deep roots and ability to go “summer dormant” during dry conditions, according to Dr. Daniel Putnam, a professor from UC Davis.

In 2014, Central Valley growers were unable to water their fields due to water scarcity, but their alfalfa generally came back to life after rainfall or irrigation resumed later in the year, he said.

Alfalfa is also a high-yielding crop, which explains why its production has remained high throughout the droughts.

Today, 99 percent of commercially grown grapes in the US come from California. With a crop value of $4.48 billion. It’s also one of the state’s top 10 priced commodities for 2020, trailing milk and almonds.

In my analysis, I found that grapes production plunged 21 percent and 13 percent in 2014 and 2015, respectively. It picked up in 2017 by 10 percent, but tumbled in 2020 by almost 27 percent.

Established grapevines are more tolerant of droughts than some fruit crops, but they do need to be watered regularly. A study found that grapes might survive if the vines are dry out by less than 50 percent. That could help explain why the crop hasn’t suffered a significant scale-back in production.

Citrus fruits such as oranges and tangerines are more drought-tolerant if they are established. The cuticle layer of their leaves help them conserve water.

As a result, farmers in Tulare County are producing more of these fruits in the past decade, as evidenced by Chart 2. The biggest increase for oranges were in 2013, when production of the fruit jumped 40 percent.

Among the 19 commodities I analyzed, orange is the most produced crop in 2013, and 2016-2020. Alfalfa was the most produced crop in 2010-2012, as well as from 2014-2015.

Tangerines experienced a 123 percent jump in production in 2014. Cultivation for the crop increased 32 percent in 2015. It fell 4 percent in 2016, but continued to grow annually from 2017 to 2019, before growth slowed down in 2020.

Thanks to factors such as the popularity of immune-building citrus fruits during the pandemic and a lower-return on dairy products, many California farmer have turned to oranges and tangerines.

The second biggest water consumer is almonds. It takes an average of 12 liters (3.2 gallons) to grow one almond, according to a recent study. Around 80 percent of the world’s almonds are grown in California.

Almonds represent 7 percent of Tulare County’s crops export in 2020. Its production surged 54 percent in 2016, when the state partially recovered from the drought.

Pomegranates are usually considered a drought resistant crop. Their production rate soared significantly during dry periods. It jumped 223 percent in 2012, 491 percent in 2015, 89 percent in 2018, and 105 percent in 2020.

Almond prices increase during dry periods

In the third chart, I took the unit price for all six crops and compared their changes over the 10 years.

Prices are usually higher for crops during drier periods since the droughts pushed the prices up for almost all commodities. The values for almonds escalated in 2014, for example.

Pomegranates requires very little water to survive, however, its production has remained pretty low. That’s probably because its value has remained pretty low throughout the years, so the incentive is not great enough for it to be grown as a major crop.
